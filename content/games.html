Games Stuff
During my gamejamming days, particularly for the three years when I actively participated in One Game a Month, I produced quite a few games, some of which some people even played. In general I've decided not to keep them all available, since I don't want to maintain decade-old game jam games but also don't want to distribute games that may no longer work correctly (or at all), but here are some highlights.
Digital Projects
Back before they set their reputation with indie devs on fire, I was game jamming and doing hobby projects primarily in Unity. There were the side scrolling basics, the stripped-down action RPGs, and the 3D experiments. One day a two-dimensional fellow found a device that let him see into a mysterious third dimension. I did a fair amount of work with integration Twine and Unity, much of which is probably obsolete now but led to some interesting projects. I made some contributions to an open source high-quality text rendering library for Unity called Typogenic to support what I was doing with Twine integration.
I came up with (as far as I know) an original tile puzzle mechanic that led to what was probably the most complete and polished project of my game jam days. I can't guarantee it will work for people, but maybe I'll keep that one available for download - [Windows], [Linux], [OSX], [Android].
I dipped my toes into a little retro gamedev, with some experiments in TRS-80 assembly programming and eventually culminating in a fairly simply but entirely playable NES game.
Tabletop Projects
I suspect no small number of budding game developers at some point realize that they could experiment with certain game mechanics more quickly if they could skip all of the time-consuming programming and asset creation and worked on a tabletop game instead. So too did my experimentation continue in the world of tokens and dice and feelies and doodads. There was the card game where you operate competing prohibition-era nightclubs, and the one where you take the roles of Fortunato and Montresor in The Cask of Amontillado.
There were board games and card games and somewhere inbetween. I designed a deck of cards with four suits which, when played against each other numerically, each had a suit they were equal to, one they were weak to, and one they were strong against, but which never really found a good game to call home. Eventually I put together a fairly ambitious attempt to recreate the game Left 4 Dead as a cards-and-dice game, which for educational purposes I even went as far as to design cards and have the game professionally printed.
